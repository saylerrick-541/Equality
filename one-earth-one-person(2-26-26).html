Meet the average
human

Strip away nationality, borders, and privilege. Based on real global data, this is the demographic center of all 8.1 billion lives.

Where you sleep
Urban area, ~570 sq ft
Slightly more than half of humans live in urban areas. Your home is shared with ~4.4 people. You likely rent, and running water is available — but not guaranteed.
Paid work
~4.4 hrs/day (all adults)
Averaged across every adult on Earth — including the employed, unemployed, retired, and part-time — the global mean is 4.4 paid hours per day. Among those who are employed, a typical shift runs 8–9 hrs, 6 days/week, usually without a contract or sick pay. The 4.4 hr average reflects that roughly half of adults at any given time are not in paid work — often because they are doing unpaid care work instead.
Unpaid care work
~3.5 hrs/day
The average person performs 3–4 hours of unpaid care daily — cooking, cleaning, childcare, elder care. Women do 2–3× more than men. This labour is worth an estimated $10–13 trillion annually yet counted as zero in GDP.
Net assets
~$122,000 (global average, all wealth)
Total global wealth — private (~$454T) and public (~$96T) — divided equally across ~4.5 billion adults gives ~$122K each. But the median adult holds just ~$8,000 in private wealth — the average is pulled far right by a tiny wealthy minority, and public assets are spread thinly or offset by sovereign debt. The bottom 50% of all adults share less than 1% of total global wealth.

Why the average feels like a lie

81% of the world's adults sit below the global average net worth of ~$122K (private + public wealth). The average is not a description of typical reality — it is an accounting of total wealth divided by heads. It is the right number for the redistribution thought experiment, because it tells you exactly what each person's mathematical share would be. But it obscures that private wealth is almost entirely held by people in the top 19%, with the top 1% alone owning ~45% of all private wealth.

Where US household wealth actually sits — by form

~32%
Home equity
The largest single asset for most Americans. You can't spend it — not without selling your home or taking on debt. For the middle 60% of households, home equity is often 60–80% of total net worth.
~26%
Retirement accounts
401(k)s, IRAs, pensions. Locked until age 59½ under penalty. 45% of American households have no retirement savings at all. The average 401(k) balance masks that the top 10% hold the majority.
~21%
Financial assets & stocks
Stocks, bonds, mutual funds. The top 1% own 54% of all corporate equity. The bottom 50% of Americans own less than 1% of the stock market. When markets rally, almost none of it reaches working people.
~21%
Business equity & other
Ownership stakes in private businesses, vehicles, other assets minus debts. Highly concentrated — mostly held by the top 20%. Includes the theoretical value of businesses that may never be sold.

The concentration problem

The top 1% of US households hold 30% of all US wealth. The top 10% hold 67%. That means the bottom 90% of Americans share just 33% of wealth — and much of their share is illiquid home equity that cannot be easily converted into food, healthcare, or financial security during a crisis. Globally, the picture is starker: the top 10% of individuals hold 76% of all private wealth on Earth. Adding public wealth (~$96T) raises the total to ~$550T but does little to shift the per-person picture: public assets are spread thinly and, in many countries, offset by sovereign debt.

What "redistribution" actually means for most people

If all global wealth — private and public combined (~$550 trillion) — were divided equally among all adults (~4.5 billion), each person would have ~$122,000. In practice, the private portion (~$454T) exists largely as real estate, corporate equity, and financial instruments — not as cash. The public portion (~$96T) exists as infrastructure and state assets that cannot simply be handed out. True redistribution would require liquidating or restructuring those assets: transferring land deeds, issuing shares, converting corporate ownership, and delivering public services universally. It is not logistically impossible. It is politically contested because those who hold the wealth also largely hold the political power to protect it.

Total global wealth — private and public combined — is approximately $550 trillion. Private wealth ($454T) is what individuals own: real estate, equities, businesses, bonds, cash. Public wealth (~$96T) is what governments hold on everyone's behalf: infrastructure, land, state enterprises, and sovereign assets, net of public debt. Below is a complete picture of what form it takes and who holds it. Sources: Credit Suisse Global Wealth Report 2023, IMF Fiscal Monitor, McKinsey Global Institute, World Bank.

The single largest store of human wealth. Equities — 18%
Shares in publicly traded companies — the stock market. Includes direct ownership, pension funds, ETFs, and mutual funds. Global market cap is ~$100T. The US stock market alone is ~$44T — nearly half the global total despite having 4% of the world's population.
WHO OWNS IT
Top 1% own 54% of all global equities. Top 10% own ~89%. The bottom 50% of the world own less than 1% of the stock market.
$77T
Private Business — 14%
Ownership stakes in businesses not listed on stock exchanges — farms, family businesses, private equity-backed companies, partnerships. This is where extreme wealth often hides: a private company has no public share price, no disclosure requirements, and its value may be theoretical until sold.
WHO OWNS IT
Extremely concentrated. Top 10% own ~85%. Includes everything from a corner shop to a $50B private equity fund. Much of billionaire wealth is here — technically enormous, practically illiquid.
$41T
Bonds & Fixed Income — 7%
Government and corporate debt instruments — bonds, treasuries, gilts. When a government issues bonds, investors buy them and receive interest payments funded by tax revenue. The interest is serviced through future tax collection.
WHO OWNS IT
Held primarily by institutions (pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds) and high-net-worth individuals. Working-class people hold bonds indirectly through pension funds, if they have them.
$27T
Cash & Deposits — 5%
Currency and bank deposits. The only asset class that is immediately accessible and spendable. Despite being just 6% of global wealth, this is what most people think of when they imagine "wealth." For the bottom 50%, this is often their only financial asset — and the balance is frequently negative.
WHO OWNS IT
Even cash is unequal: the top 10% hold ~55% of global deposits. Large cash holdings are themselves an investment strategy for the ultra-wealthy — parked in high-yield accounts earning passive income unavailable to those living paycheck to paycheck.
~$96T
Public Wealth — 17%
Net government assets: infrastructure, public land, state-owned enterprises, sovereign wealth funds — minus public debt. Roads, hospitals, schools, water systems, national parks, and public housing all count here. This is the wealth held collectively, in theory on behalf of everyone. In practice it has been steadily shrinking since the 1980s as privatisation transferred public assets into the private column.
WHO OWNS IT
Everyone — in theory. But access to public goods is deeply unequal across and within countries. Net public wealth has turned negative in many advanced economies when sovereign debt is factored in. Norway ($1.4T sovereign fund) and China (vast state enterprises) are outliers. Most governments' public wealth is essentially zero or negative once debt is counted.

The stock market fallacy

Because the bottom 90% own less than 11% of all equities globally, a 20% market rally adds ~$18 trillion to the top 10%'s net worth — roughly the entire GDP of China — while adding less than $2 trillion to the bottom 90% combined.

The liquidity trap

Most global wealth is illiquid — you cannot eat a share certificate or pay rent with an acre of land. Even the equal-share figure (~$122K per adult including public wealth, ~$100K private only) would be largely inaccessible without structural reorganisation: converting property rights, liquidating equity, transferring business ownership, and ensuring universal access to public assets. The wealth is real. The access is not.

Part III — The Invisible Economy

Care, health & education
— the unmeasured world

These three domains form the foundation of every human society. They are also the three most underfunded, underpaid, and systematically undervalued sectors on Earth — and disproportionately performed by women.

$10–13 trillion in unpaid work happens every year.
The ILO estimates unpaid care work — raising children, caring for the elderly, maintaining households — represents between 10–39% of GDP in high-income countries, and would rank as the world's largest economic sector if counted. It is invisible in every national account. It is performed overwhelmingly by women. And in the equal world model, its fair valuation and redistribution between genders may be the single largest structural change.

"Equal distribution" here means one specific thing: all work — paid and unpaid — redistributed equally between men and women. The total hours of work done globally don't change. Reflection

We have enough.
Always have.

The data is clear: scarcity is not the problem. The Earth produces sufficient food, energy, water, and shelter for every person alive. What we face is a problem of distribution — maintained by borders, governance structures, inheritance, and historical accumulation. And beneath all of it, an entire economy of care, healing, and teaching that props up every other system while being counted as worth nothing. The average human is not living poorly because resources are scarce. They are living poorly because those resources — and the labor that sustains them — were captured and rendered invisible before they were born.
